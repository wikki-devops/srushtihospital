Bicornuate Uterus

What Is a Bicornuate Uterus?

It’s a type of congenital uterine anomaly where the womb is heart-shaped or divided — instead of a single unified space. Many women don’t even know they have it until they face miscarriages or difficulty conceiving.

Stats in India

This rare condition affects about 1 in 200 women. Most discover it during evaluations for repeated miscarriage or IVF failure.

Treatment

Mild cases need only close monitoring. In some, surgery (metroplasty) may be done to unify the uterine space — especially if recurrent pregnancy loss occurs.

Why IVF Sometimes Helps

If natural conception isn’t working or miscarriage risk is high, IVF with close monitoring gives the best outcome.

Frequently Asked Questions About Bicornuate Uterus

Can I get pregnant naturally?

Yes, many women with a bicornuate uterus conceive naturally and have healthy pregnancies.

Will I need surgery?

Only in select cases — surgery is considered if there’s a history of recurrent miscarriages or poor pregnancy outcomes.

Is it dangerous?

With proper care and monitoring, a bicornuate uterus is manageable and not inherently dangerous.

Can it cause miscarriage?

Yes, in some cases. Close follow-up with a fertility specialist or obstetrician is key for managing risks.

Can I carry full-term?

With medical support and regular monitoring, many women with a bicornuate uterus carry to full term successfully.

Is IVF successful?

Yes. IVF can be successful in women with a bicornuate uterus, especially with careful embryo placement and follow-up.

Can I have twins?

It’s riskier due to limited space in the uterus. That’s why we often recommend transferring only one embryo during IVF.

Will my baby be normal?

Absolutely. The uterine shape may affect pregnancy, but it does not affect your baby’s development or health.

Can it be diagnosed early?

Yes. It can be identified through advanced imaging like a transvaginal ultrasound, 3D scan, or MRI.

Can it be fixed permanently?

Surgical correction, like metroplasty, can help in select cases with recurrent loss or complications — but is not always necessary.
