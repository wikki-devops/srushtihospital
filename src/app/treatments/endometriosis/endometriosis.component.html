Endometriosis

What Is Endometriosis?

Polyps are soft overgrowths of the inner lining of the uterus (endometrium). They’re like tiny obstacles that sometimes prevent implantation or cause bleeding between periods.

Treatment

If the disease has severely affected the ovaries or tubes, or if age is a concern, IVF bypasses the damage and improves chances dramatically.

Frequently Asked Questions About Endometriosis

Is it a lifelong condition?

It can recur over time, but with proper treatment and monitoring, it is manageable and symptoms can be controlled.

Does it cause infertility?

In some women, yes. It can affect egg quality, tube function, or the uterus — but many go on to conceive successfully.

Is surgery always needed?

Not always. Mild cases can often be managed with medication and lifestyle changes. Surgery is recommended if symptoms are severe or fertility is affected.

Does pregnancy cure it?

Pregnancy may reduce symptoms temporarily, but it does not cure endometriosis. Management is still important afterward.

Will it affect egg quality?

Possibly. Endometriosis can have an impact on ovarian reserve, especially if cysts (endometriomas) are present on the ovaries.

Can it spread?

It doesn’t spread like cancer, but it can grow in other areas of the pelvis like ovaries, fallopian tubes, or bowels.

Is it cancerous?

No. Endometriosis is a benign condition. The chances of it turning into cancer are extremely rare.

Does diet help?

Yes. Anti-inflammatory diets rich in fruits, vegetables, and omega-3s may help reduce flare-ups and improve overall well-being.

Is IVF always necessary?

Not always. Many women conceive naturally or through simpler treatments. IVF is advised only when other methods don’t work or in advanced cases.

Can I live pain-free?

With a personalized treatment plan, lifestyle adjustments, and expert care — yes, living pain-free is absolutely possible for many women.
